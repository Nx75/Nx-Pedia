Background

Linear motion (rectilinear motion) is one‑dimensional motion along a straight line, described using a single spatial coordinate. Aristotle distinguished between natural and violent motion; he argued that heavy bodies seek their natural place. Galileo later overturned Aristotelian ideas through experiments with inclined planes, showing that in vacuum all bodies fall with the same constant acceleration. In the 17th century, Newton formalised the laws of motion, establishing that a body remains in uniform linear motion unless acted upon by a force. The study of linear motion forms the foundation of kinematics and is essential for understanding Newton’s second law: F = m a along the line of motion.

Rectilinear motion can be uniform (constant velocity) or non‑uniform (varying velocity). The geometry is the simplest in physics: position x(t) is a function of time, and all other kinematic quantities are derived from it. Historically, the description of free fall as constant‑acceleration motion was a breakthrough, leading to the general equations of motion used today.

Displacement

Displacement (Δx) is the vector that points from the initial position to the final position. For motion along a straight line, its magnitude is the absolute difference of the coordinates, and its sign indicates direction. Unlike distance (a scalar), displacement includes directional information. For example, a particle moving from x = +5 m to x = –3 m undergoes a displacement Δx = –8 m. In differential form, infinitesimal displacement is written dx, and the total displacement over a time interval is the integral ∫ v dt.

Displacement is independent of the path; in one dimension the path is the line segment itself, so displacement magnitude equals distance only if motion never reverses. When analysing motion graphs, the area under the velocity‑time curve gives displacement.

Velocity

Average velocity is defined as displacement divided by elapsed time: vavg = Δx / Δt. Instantaneous velocity is the limit of this ratio as Δt → 0, i.e. the derivative v = dx/dt. Velocity is a vector; its sign indicates direction along the chosen axis. In uniform linear motion, velocity remains constant; for accelerated motion it varies. Speed is the magnitude of velocity.

Uniform linear motionv = constant

Position changes linearly: x(t) = x₀ + v t. Acceleration is zero. Example: a train moving at 80 km/h on a straight track.

Instantaneous velocityv = dx/dt

Derived from the slope of the position–time graph. For non‑linear x(t), velocity varies with time.

For a round trip with returns, average velocity may be zero while average speed is positive.

Acceleration

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity. Average acceleration: aavg = Δv / Δt. Instantaneous acceleration: a = dv/dt = d²x/dt². In one dimension, positive acceleration means velocity is increasing in the positive direction; negative acceleration (deceleration) reduces velocity. For constant acceleration, the equations of motion simplify considerably. An important special case is free fall near Earth’s surface, where a = –g = –9.81 m/s² (if upward is positive).

Acceleration can also be expressed as a = v (dv/dx), which is useful when time is not directly involved. In mechanics, the net force along the line determines acceleration via Newton’s second law.

Formulation & equations of motion

For constant acceleration (the most common idealisation in 1D problems), the following kinematic relations hold. They are derived from the definitions of velocity and acceleration by integration, assuming a = constant.

1st equationv = v₀ + a t

velocity as a function of time.

2nd equationx = x₀ + v₀ t + ½ a t²

position as a function of time.

3rd equationv² = v₀² + 2a (x – x₀)

time‑independent relation, useful when t is unknown.

These equations are valid only for constant acceleration. For variable acceleration, one must integrate: v(t) = v₀ + ∫ a(t) dt and x(t) = x₀ + ∫ v(t) dt. In the case of simple harmonic motion (e.g., mass‑spring), acceleration is proportional to negative displacement, leading to sinusoidal solutions.

Free fall – 1D motion under gravity (constant acceleration)

Near Earth’s surface and neglecting air resistance, free fall is rectilinear motion with constant acceleration g = 9.81 m/s² downward. Choosing upward as positive, a = –g. The equations become:

v = v₀ – g t
y = y₀ + v₀ t – ½ g t²
v² = v₀² – 2g (y – y₀)

Maximum height (when v = 0): h = v₀²/(2g). Total time of flight (if launch and landing at same height): T = 2v₀/g. Galileo famously deduced that all objects fall with the same acceleration by rolling balls on inclined planes (which effectively “dilute” gravity).

Comparison to rotational motion

Linear motion differs fundamentally from rotational motion. In rotation, every particle moves in a circle about an axis; quantities like angular displacement (θ), angular velocity (ω = dθ/dt), and angular acceleration (α = dω/dt) replace the linear variables. There is a direct mathematical analogy: θ ↔ x, ω ↔ v, α ↔ a, and the rotational equations for constant α are ω = ω₀ + α t, θ = θ₀ + ω₀ t + ½ α t². However, rotational motion also involves centripetal acceleration (ac = ω² r) directed toward the axis, which has no counterpart in rectilinear motion. In pure linear motion, acceleration is always parallel or anti‑parallel to velocity.

Another distinction: in rotation, different points on a rigid body have different linear velocities but the same angular velocity. The background of linear motion is often taught first because it introduces kinematic concepts without the complication of circular geometry.

Further aspects of 1D motion: In addition to free fall, examples include a car braking uniformly, a train speeding up with constant acceleration, or an object thrown vertically upward. The sign convention (which direction is positive) must be stated clearly. Problems often involve two objects meeting, or motion with piecewise constant acceleration (e.g., a rocket that accelerates, then coasts). Graphical analysis: slope of x‑t gives velocity, slope of v‑t gives acceleration, area under v‑t gives displacement, area under a‑t gives change in velocity. For non‑constant acceleration, numerical integration may be required.

Modern applications: particle accelerators (linear motion of charged particles), elevators, conveyor belts, and any system that can be approximated as moving along a straight line. The concepts also extend to general curvilinear motion by considering tangential and normal components, but the one‑dimensional treatment remains the core building block.
